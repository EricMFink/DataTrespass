## NLRA Pre-Emption

The pre-emptive sweep of federal labor law is particularly broad.^[{ | San Diego Building Trades Council v. Garmon, 359 U.S. 236 1959 | | | zu:21885:ABU98E47 }] State law may not regulate any activity that is arguably protected or prohibited under the NLRA.^[{ | Machinists v. Wisconsin Employment Relations Commn, 427 U.S. 132 1976 | | | zu:21885:S3P4WMWW }; { | San Diego Building Trades Council v. Garmon, 359 U.S. 236 1959 | | | zu:21885:ABU98E47 }] Pre-emption applies to state laws imposing criminal or civil liability on employees for activity that is either protected or prohibited under the NLRA.^[CITES] 

Accordingly, if an employer's NC PPA suit against an employee is based on conduct that is arguably protected under the NLRA, Garmon preemption will apply. A state court may not decide whether the conduct is indeed protected, as that question is within the exclusive original jurisdiction of the NLRB. Rather, as long as there is any colorable argument for NLRA protection, the state court should dismiss (or stay its proceedings pending a decision by the NLRB). 

### Procedure for Asserting Garmon Preemption 

"A claim of Garmon preemption is a claim that the state court has no power to adjudicate the subject matter of the case, and when a claim of Garmon preemption is raised, it must be considered and resolved by the state court.""^[{ | International Longshoremen's Ass'n. v. Davis, 476 US 380 Supreme Court, 1986 | | | zu:21885:GNYTJ86Z } at 393] Because Garmon preemption is asserted as a defense, it not a basis for removal of a suit asserting only state law claims.^[{ | Bud Antle, Inc. v. Barbosa, 45 F. 3d 1261 1994 | | | zu:21885:RYR9H9K5 }]. But, since Garmon preemption is a question of federal law, "if the state court errs in determining whether Garmon priciples deprive it of jurisdiction over a dispute, review of that decision may be had in the Supreme Court."^[{ | Bud Antle, Inc. v. Barbosa, 45 F. 3d 1261 1994 | | | zu:21885:RYR9H9K5 } at 1269, quoting e{ | Ethridge v. Harbor House Restaurant, 861 F. 2d 1389 1988 | | | zu:21885:LWPQAE7E } at 1399. ]

However, the employee may obtain more immediate relief from the NLRB or a federal court. It is an unfair labor practice for an employer to maintain a state court lawsuit against an employee or union for protected concerted activity. ^[{ | Can-Am Plumbing, Inc. v. NLRB, 321 F. 3d 145 Court of Appeals, Dist. of Columbia Circuit, 2003 | | | zu:21885:664DNBRN } at 151; { | Associated Building Contractors, Inc., 331 NLRB 132 2000 | | | zu:21885:P777PN5M }; { | Manno Elec., Inc., 321 NLRB 297 1996 | | | zu:21885:2YXG75ZI } at 297.] Accordingly, an employee faced with such a suit may file an unfair labor practice charge with the NLRB, which may then order the employer to seek dismissal or a stay (pending the NLRB's disposition of the unfair labor practice charge) of the state court action.^[CITES] The NLRB may also bring an action in a federal district court seeking an injunction for that purpose.^[{ | Can-Am Plumbing, Inc. v. NLRB, 321 F. 3d 145 Court of Appeals, Dist. of Columbia Circuit, 2003 | | | zu:21885:664DNBRN } at 151, citing { | Bill Johnson's Restaurants, Inc. v. NLRB, 461 US 731 Supreme Court, 1983 | | | zu:21885:WLBTEPK3 } at 744; { | NLRB v. Nash-Finch Co., 404 US 138 Supreme Court, 1971 | | | zu:21885:AU79NSIA } at 144.] Alternatively, the employee may bring an action in federal court seeking declaratory and injunctive relief to block the state court action.^[{ | Bud Antle, Inc. v. Barbosa, 45 F. 3d 1261 1994 | | | zu:21885:RYR9H9K5 } (holding employer may obtain declaratory and injunctive relief in federal court against pending state administrative agency proceeding).] BUT DO THE ANTI-INJUNCTION ACT OR YOUNGER ABSTENTION APPLY WHERE A PRIVATE PARTY SEEKS TO ENJOIN A STATE COURT SUIT, RATHER THAN A STATE ADMINISTRATIVE PROCEEDING (AS IN BUD ANTLE)?
